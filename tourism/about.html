Juan de Salcedo accidentally discovered Lubang on May 08, 1570. A year later, it was placed in a Spanish rule under Miguel Lopez de Legaspi, the first Spanish Governor General. From then on, Spanish settlements were established in the island.

In 1574 Lubang became annex of Mindoro as a part of the newly created corrigamiento of Bonbon currently known as Batangas. In 1896, Lubang joined with its motherland, Cavite, in the uprising against Spanish Government. It is a home of revolutionary leaders, which includes Emilio Cajayon, Esteban Quijano, Toribio Aguilar and others.

With the passage of Republic Act No. 423 on June 23, 1902, Lubang was detached from Cavite and became a part of Marinduque. But after five (5) months, it became a part of Mindoro with the passage of Act No.500 on November 10, 1902.

Under Act No. 1280 of the Philippine Commission on June 4, 1905, Lubang was made a separate town with Tilik as seat of government. Looc was made a separate town in 1916.

Lubang was the first town in Mindoro where the SVD (German priest) established its first mission in 1921.

Lubang Island was also known as the Island of Illustrados because it has produced professionals among the early inhabitants of Mindoro before its division in 1953.

In terms of trade, Lubang Island is the gateway to Manila. Hence, vessels going to Romblon and other nearly Visayan Provinces drop their anchors first in Tilik port, and the last port before proceeding to manila.

Lubang Island received international attention in 1974 when Lt. Hiroo Onoda, a Japanese straggler who didn’t know that World War II had ended, emerged from the hills of this Island after 30 years in the forest. He returned to Japan a hero, especially in his hometown of Wakayama Prefecture. With the sighting of the former Japanese Imperial Army, Lt. Onoda, the island was thrown into limelight since then. This even generated and developed consciousness of the island internationally, particularly among Japanese.
